Structural health monitoring (SHM)

  1. The process of implementing a damage detection and characterization strategy for engineering structures.
  2. Damage is defined as changes to the material and/or geometric properties of a structural system, including changes to the boundary conditions and system connectivity, which adversely affect the system's performance.
  3. The SHM process involves the observation of a system over time using periodically sampled response measurements from an array of sensors (often inertial accelerometers), the extraction of damage-sensitive features from these measurements, and the statistical analysis of these features to determine the current state of system health.
  4. Long term SHM, the output of this process is periodically updated information regarding the ability of the structure to perform its intended function in light of the inevitable aging and degradation resulting from operational environments.
  5. The process of implementing a damage detection and characterization strategy for engineering structures.
  6. After extreme events, such as earthquakes or blast loading, SHM is used for rapid condition screening and aims to provide, in near real time, reliable information regarding the integrity of the structure.
  7. As part of the rapid developments in data-driven technologies, that are transforming many fields in engineering and science, machine learning and computer vision techniques are increasingly capable of reliably diagnosing and classifying patterns in image data, which has clear applications in inspection contexts.

Operational modal analysis

  1. The field of engineering that studies the modal properties of systems under ambient or normal operating conditions.
  2. Modal properties are considered to be natural frequencies, damping and mode shapes.
  3. Excitation is not known yet is assumed to have, approximately, white noise characteristics.
  4. Modal characteristics can be used to identify basic properties (geometric, material, etc.) of the structure. Usually this is accomplished by correlating the dynamic characteristics of a mathematical model with physical properties derived from experimental measurements.

Stretching method in the SHM context

  • The Stretching Method (SM) is widely used in passive imaging applications mostly related to Geophysics
  • The key idea of the method is to estimate how much we need to stretch a signal, referred to as current, so as to maximize its correlation coefficient with another signal, the reference.
  • Let \(u^{(k)}(x_l)\) and \(u^{(m)}(x_n)\) be the recordings of the response in the \(k^{\text{th}}\) and \(m^{\text{th}}\) directions at locations \(x_l\) and \(x_n\), respectively. Combination of direction and location defines a specific dof, \(u^{(k)}(x_l){=}u_i\) and \(u^{(m)}(x_n){=}u_j\). The empirical cross-correlation function over time interval \([0,T]\): \({C_{ij}}_T(\tau) = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T u_i(t) u_j(t+\tau) \textrm{d}t \stackrel{T \to \infty}{\longrightarrow} {C_{ij}}^{\textrm{av}} (\tau)= \left< {C_{ij}}_{\tilde{T}}(\tau) \right>\)
  • In the SHM context, SM is not applied directly to the time domain cross-correlations but rather to their Fourier transforms: \(A^\#_{ij}(\nu) = \int {C_{ij}}_T(\tau) e^{\imath 2 \pi \tau \nu} d\tau,\, \#=r,\,c\)
  • The matrix \(A^\#(\nu)\) is symmetric so it admits a symmetric singular value decomposition (SVD), \(A^\#(\nu) = U^\#(\nu)\Sigma^\#(\nu)U^{^\#,T}(\nu)\)
  • The frequencies at which the first singular value admit peaks correspond to the natural frequencies of the structure while the first singular vector at the corresponding frequency is identified as the modal shape associated to this natural frequency.
  • To monitor variations in the natural frequency \(\nu\), SM maximizes the correlation coefficient \(C_s(\Delta \nu)=\dfrac{\int_{\nu_1}^{\nu_2} \sigma_1^c(\nu+\Delta \nu) \sigma_1^r(\nu) \textrm{d}\nu}{\sqrt{\int_{\nu_1}^{\nu_2} (\sigma_1^c(\nu+\Delta \nu))^2 \textrm{d}\nu \int_{\nu_1}^{\nu_2} (\sigma_1^r(\nu))^2 \textrm{d}\nu}}.\)
  • SM shown to be less sensitive to environmental fluctuations, amenable to direct integration within smart sensors and embedded electronics.
- C. Tsogka, E. Daskalakis, G. Comanducci, F. Ubertini, The stretching method for vibration-based structural health monitoring of civil structures., Computer-Aided Civil and Infrastructure Engineering, Vol. 32, Issue 4, pp. 288–303, 2017.

Model updating

  • Sensitivity-based element-by-element updating procedure
  • Spatial distribution using piecewise linear functions, defined as:\(\psi_s(x)=\left(H[x-x_{s-1}]-H[x-x_{s}]\right)\frac{x-x_{s-1}}{\Delta x}+\left(H[x-x_{s}]-H[x-x_{s+1}]\right)\frac{\Delta x-x-x_{s}}{\Delta x},\)
  • common for elastic (Young's modulus) and inertial (mass density) properties: \(E(x)=E_0\sum\limits_{s=1}^S a_s \psi_s(x)\text{ and }\rho(x)=\rho_0\sum\limits_{s=1}^S a_s \psi_s(x).\)
  • Objective function \(f\) to be minimized over the \(S\) design variables \(a_s\): \(f(a_s)=(\nu_1(a_s)-\tilde{\nu}_1)^2+\left(\frac{\nu_2(a_s)}{\nu_1(a_s)}-\tilde{r}_{21}\right)^2\).
  • Use of the limited-memory BFGS gradient algorithm, needing derivative of the objective function \(f\) with respect to \(a_s\): \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial a_s}=2(\nu_1-\tilde{\nu}_1)\frac{\partial \nu_1}{\partial a_s}+\frac{2}{\nu_1^3}(\tilde{r}_{21}\nu_1-\nu_2)(\nu_2\frac{\partial \nu_1}{\partial a_s}-\nu_1\frac{\partial \nu_2}{\partial a_s})\),
  • where \(\frac{\partial \nu_i}{\partial a_s}\) the sensitivity indexes for the eigenfrequencies. The dependence of a given eigenfrequency \(\omega_i{=}2\pi\nu_i\) on design variable \(a_s\):\(\frac{\partial \omega_i}{\partial a_s}=\frac{1}{2\omega_i m_i^{\ast}}\phi_i^T\left(\frac{\partial K}{\partial a_s}-\omega_i^2 \frac{\partial M}{\partial a_s} \right)\phi_i\)
  • Finally we take advantage of sensitivities on a set of variables (i.e., \(\frac{\partial K}{\partial E},\frac{\partial K}{\partial \rho}\) and \(\frac{\partial M}{\partial E},\frac{\partial M}{\partial \rho}\)) given by the mathematical (finite element) model: \(\frac{\partial \omega_i}{\partial \alpha_s}=\frac{\partial \omega_i}{\partial E}\frac{\partial E}{\partial \alpha_s}+\frac{\partial \omega_i}{\partial \rho}\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial \alpha_s}=\frac{1}{2\omega_i m_i^{\ast}}\phi_i^T\left(\frac{\partial K}{\partial E}-\omega_i^2 \frac{\partial M}{\partial E} \right)\phi_i\frac{\partial E}{\partial a_s}+\frac{1}{2\omega_i m_i^{\ast}}\phi_i^T\left(\frac{\partial K}{\partial \rho}-\omega_i^2 \frac{\partial M}{\partial \rho} \right)\phi_i\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial a_s}\)

Computational time reversal for SHM

  • Time reversal can be used to solve certain classes of inverse wave problems.
  • It is based on the reversibility property of wave propagation phenomena in nondissipative media.
  • An important consequence of this property is that one can "time-reverse" developed signals, by letting them propagate back in time to the location of the source that emitted them originally. If the original source is very local, this process is called refocusing.
  • It can be used for the solution of other types of inverse problems, including obstacle (scatterer) identification, damage identification in structures (nondestructive testing) and undersurface geophysical structure identification.
  • In the presence of damping it has been numerically observed that still time reversal can be utilized with possible delay of refocusing.
\(\scriptscriptstyle \begin{align} \kappa GA\left(\frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial^2 v}{\partial x^2}\right)+\rho A \frac{\partial^2 v}{\partial t^2}{=}w(x,t),\quad \kappa GA\left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}-\psi\right)+EI\frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x^2}-\rho I\frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial t^2}{=}\mu(x,t). \end{align}\)

Forward problem

  • From some source point a pulse excitation is emitted at time \(t_0\)
  • Response is captured on a finite number of receivers for total duration time \(T\)
  • Each receiver is defined by its location and direction of recording (d.o.f.)

Backward problem

  • The signal (response) on each receiver is reversed in time
  • The reversed in time signal is then re-emitted as excitation
  • Refocusing happens at time \(T{-}t_0\)
  • Refocusing appeared on the location of the source

Acoustic Emission in Elastic Medium

- Tomáš Roubíček, Christos G. Panagiotopoulos, Chrysoula Tsogka, Explicit time-discretisation of elastodynamics with some inelastic processes at small strains, (Preprint arXiv no.1903.11654. ) SIAM J. Numer. Anal., submitted.

Acoustic Emission

  1. The study of elastic waves, involving linear elasticity with variation in time, is called elastodynamics.
  2. An elastic wave is a type of mechanical wave that propagates in elastic or viscoelastic materials.
  3. When they occur in the earth as the result of an earthquake or other disturbance, elastic waves are usually called seismic waves.
  4. Extension to nonlinear waves in elastic or inelastic media is possible and always a challenge, both from computational and analysis viewpoints.
The goal here is to define an appropriate numerical scheme capable to solve problems of wave propagation in elastic medium considering possible dissipative processes on the boundary (e.g. surface damage, crack).
Implementation of such an algorithm provides us with a computational tool in order to numerically solve problems of Acoustic Emission.
Acoustic emission is the phenomenon of radiation of acoustic (elastic) waves in solids that occurs when a material undergoes irreversible changes in its internal structure.
The waves generated by acoustic emission are of practical interest in SHM, where is typically used to detect, locate and characterize damage.

Second order formulation of Elastodynamics

The initial-boundary-value problem in mind is the linear elastodynamic at small strains:
\(\begin{align} &\rho\ddot{u}-{\rm div}\mathbb{C} e(u)=f &&\text{on }\ \varOmega\ \text{ for }\ t\in[0,T],\\ &[\mathbb{C} e(u)]\vec{n}+\mathbb{B} u=g&&\text{on }\ \varGamma\ \text{ for }\ t\in[0,T],\\ &u|_{t=0}=u_0,\ \ \ \dot{u}|_{t=0}=v_0&&\text{on }\ \varOmega, \end{align}\)
with \(\varrho>0\) a mass density, \(\mathbb{C}\) a symmetric positive definite 4th-order elasticity tensor, \(e(u)=\frac{1}{2}(\nabla u)^\top{+}\frac{1}{2}\nabla u\) the small-strain tensor, \(\mathbb{B}\) a symmetric positive semidefinite 2nd-order tensor determining the elastic support on the boundary, \(f\) the bulk force, \(g\) the surface loading, \(u_0\) the prescribed initial displacement, \(v_0\) initial velocity, and \(T>0\) a fixed time horizon.
The above set of equations in abstract form:
\(\begin{align} \mathcal{T}'\ddot{u}+\mathcal{W}' u=\mathcal{F_u}'(t)\ \ \text{ for }\ t\in[0,T],\ \ u|_{t=0}=u_0,\ \ \ \dot{u}|_{t=0}=v_0, \end{align}\)
where \(\mathcal{T}\) is the kinetic energy, \(\mathcal W\) is the stored energy, and \(\mathcal F\) is the external force, while \((\cdot)'\) denotes the Gateaux derivative.

First order formulation of Elastodynamics

We rewrite equations of elastodynamics in the velocity/stress formulation, i.e. terms of \(v=\dot{u}\) and of the stress \(\sigma=\mathbb{C} e(u)\), eliminating the displacement \(u\), further we consider the rate form of the surface equation together with appropriate initial conditions:
\(\begin{align} &\rho\dot{v}-{\rm div}\sigma=f &&\text{on }\ \varOmega\ \text{ for }\ t\in[0,T],\\ &\dot{\sigma}\vec{n}+\mathbb{B} v=\dot{g}&&\text{on }\ \varGamma\ \text{ for }\ t\in[0,T],\\ &v|_{t=0}=v_0,\ \ \ \sigma|_{t=0}=\sigma_0&&\text{on }\ \varOmega, \end{align}\)
after having establish some solution for the velocity field \(v\), if necessary, we can then define the respective displacement field \(u\) by integrating in time velocities.
In the abstract form, when writing \(\mathcal W=\mathscr{W}\circ E\) with \(E\) denoting the linear operator \(u\mapsto(e,w):=(e(u),u|_\varGamma)\):
\(\begin{align} &&&\mathcal T'\dot{v}+E^*\varSigma=F(t)&&\text{ for }\ t\in[0,T],&&v|_{t=0}^{}=v_0,\ \ \ \ \text{ and }\ \ \\&&&\dot{\varSigma}=\mathscr{W}'Ev+\dot{G}(t) &&\text{ for }\ t\in[0,T], &&\varSigma|_{t=0}^{}=\varSigma_0:=\mathscr{W}'Eu_0,&&&& \end{align}\)
where the "generalized" stress \(\varSigma=\mathscr{W}'Eu=(\mathbb{B} e(u),\mathbb{B} u|_\varGamma)\) contains, beside the bulk stress tensor, also the traction stress vector.

Internal variables and their dissipative evolution

The concept of internal variables has a long tradition and opens wide options for material modelling while the internal parameters are subjected to 1st-order evolution flow rules. The system equations, in the 1st order velocity/proto-stress formulation, is thus enhanced as:
\(\begin{align} &\dot{\varSigma}=\mathfrak CEv+\dot{G}(t) && \text{ for }\ t\in[0,T],\ \ && \\ &\mathcal T'\dot{v}+E^*S =F(t)\ \ \text{ with }\ \ S=\mathfrak C^*\varPhi_\varSigma'(\varSigma,z)\!\!\!\!\!\!&& \text{ for }\ t\in[0,T],\ \ \\ &\partial\varPsi(\dot z)+\varPhi_z'(\varSigma,z) \ni0&& \text{ for }\ t\in[0,T],\\ &\varSigma|_{t=0}=\varSigma_0:=\mathfrak CEu_0+G(0)\,,\ \ \ v|_{t=0}=v_0\,, \ \ z|_{t=0}=z_0. \end{align}\)
Where \(\mathfrak C\) stands for a "generalized" elasticity tensor.
The inclusion (\(\ni\)) refers to a possibility that the convex (pseudo)potential of dissipative forces \(\varPsi\) may be nonsmooth and then its subdifferential \(\partial\varPsi\) can be multivalued.
\(\varSigma\) may not directly enter the balance of forces and is thus to be called rather as some "proto-stress", while the actual generalized stress will be denoted by \(S\).
\(\varPhi_\varSigma'(\varSigma,z)\) is in a position of a "generalized" strain and, when multiplied by \(\mathfrak C^*\), it becomes a generalized stress.

A three-step staggered time discretisation

we devise the leap-frog discretisation of the first two equations combined with the fractional-step split (a staggered scheme) with a mid-point formula for the third one.
  1. calculate \(\varSigma_{\tau h}^{k+1}\):\(\frac{\varSigma_{\tau h}^{k+1}-\varSigma_{\tau h}^{k}}\tau= \mathfrak CE_h v_{\tau h}^{k}+D_{\tau h}^{k}\)
  2. calculate \(z_{\tau h}^{k+1}\):\(\partial\varPsi\Big(\frac{z_{\tau h}^{k+1}{-}z_{\tau h}^{k}}\tau\Big)+\varPhi_z'\Big(\varSigma_{\tau h}^{k+1},\frac{z_{\tau h}^{k+1}{+}z_{\tau h}^{k}}{2}\Big)\ni0\)
  3. calculate \(v_{\tau h}^{k+1}\):\(\mathcal{T}'\frac{v_{\tau h}^{k+1}{-}v_{\tau h}^{k}}{\tau}+E_h^\ast S_{\tau h}^{k+1}=F_{\tau h}^{k+1} \ \text{ with }\ S_{\tau h}^{k+1}=\mathfrak C^*\varPhi_\varSigma'(\varSigma_{\tau h}^{k+1},z_{\tau h}^{k+1})\)
  4. and \(u_{\tau h}^{k+1}\):

    \(u_{\tau h}^{k+1}=u_{\tau h}^{k}+\tau v_{\tau h}^{k+1}\)

Stress Criterion for fracture of Linear Elastic-Brittle Materials

  • Principal stresses - (real) eigenvalues of symmetric stress tensor \(\sigma_{ij}(x)\) for \(x \in \Omega\): \(\sigma_1(x)\ge\sigma_2(x)\ge\sigma_3(x)\).
  • Stress criterion (the maximum principal stress criterion) predicts breakage at a point \(x \in \Omega\) if \(\sigma_1(x) \ge \sigma_c\), where \(\sigma_c\) the tensile strength.
  • This stress criterion works quite well in many practical situations.

However ...

  1. Predicting fracture at a point for a given load, the size of the new crack is not defined
  2. Is not able to predict fracture at a stress singularity, where \(\sigma\rightarrow\pm\infty\)
  3. Any small load leads to fracture at a singular point according to the stress criterion
  4. Is not able to appropriately consider size effects

Energy Criterion for fracture of Linear Elastic-Brittle Materials

  • The solution for displacements \(u(x)\) is minimizer for the Potential energy: \(\Pi=\int_{\Omega}C_{ijkl}(x)u(x)_{i,j}u(x)_{k,l}\text{d}x-\int_{\Omega}f_i(x)u_i(x)\text{d}x-\int_{\partial \Omega_t}\tilde{t}_i(x)u_i(x)\text{d}s(x)\)
  • We may say that the above potential energy refers to a specific crack length \(\alpha\), that is \(\Pi(\alpha)\)
  • Fracture energy (fracture toughness) \(G_c\) is the specific energy required to create a new crack surface of unit area
  • Dissipated energy \(R(\alpha)= G_c \alpha\) required to create a new crack of area \(\alpha\) (the simplest situation of opening fracture mode I)
  • Griffith energy criterion (1st Law of Thermodynamics): a crack can propagate by an infinitesimal increase of its area \(\text{d}\alpha\) if a sufficient amount of potential energy is released \((\Pi\searrow \text{ for }\alpha\nearrow)\)
  • The energy release rate \(G(\alpha)=-\frac{\partial \Pi}{\partial \alpha}(\alpha)\ge\frac{\partial R}{\partial \alpha}(\alpha)\Leftrightarrow \frac{\partial (\Pi+R)}{\partial \alpha}(\alpha)\le0\)
  • Griffth's energy criterion, for an infinitesimal crack advance, has many practical situations

However, does not predict well:

  1. propagation of short cracks
  2. crack onset at stress concentrations
  3. crack onset at weak stress singularities

Coupled Stress-Energy Criterion

Several solutions to the above diffculties of both stress and energy criteria considered separately were proposed in the past (Theory of Critical Distances (TCD), Cohesize Zone Models (CZM), Energetic Solution (ES),etc.) - no one satisfactorily solves the problem of fracture onset in brittle materials

  1. The Coupled Stress-Energy Criterion of Finite Fracture Mechanics (CC-FFM) is based on two hypotheses:
    • Hashin's (1996) hypothesis of a finite-crack onset, \(\Delta a > 0\). Time scale of crack onset is much shorter than the time scale where the whole structural problem under consideration is solved, i.e. from the point view of FFM the crack onset is instantaneous
    • Leguillon's (2002) hypothesis that a necessary and sufficient condition for such a finite-crack onset \(\Delta a > 0\) is the simultaneous fulfillment of both stress & (incremental) energy criteria
  2. Into the present framework [1]:
    • Formulate the CC-FFM as an optimization problem
    • Setting up a general algorithm for numerical applications
    • Implementation for BEM analysis
[1] Work still in progress, in collaboration with prof. Vladislav Mantic, University of Seville.

Principle of Minimum Total Energy subject to a Stress Condition (PMTE-SC)

  • An alternative formulation of the CC-FFM has been proposed [1] to apply Principle of Minimum Total Energy subject to a Stress Condition (PMTE-SC) to the problem of crack onset and propagation for a given applied load (assuming quasistatic loading, and neglecting inertial forces): \(\begin{align} &\text{minimize }\Pi(\Delta S_c)+R(\Delta S_c)\\ &\text{subject to stress condition} \end{align}\)
  • \(\Pi(\Delta S_c)\) - the potential energy for the given load and the considered new crack surface \(\Delta S_c\).
  • \(R(\Delta S_c)\) - the total energy dissipated including the formation of this new crack surface \(\Delta S_c\).
  • The stress constraint means that the total energy minimization considers only those new crack surfaces for which the stress criterion is verified for a given load.
  • The stress criterion defines a condition on stresses at the potential new crack surface(s) of a finite (i.e. non-infinitesimal) extension. A quite general formulation can be written as: \(f(\sigma_{ij}(\cdot), \Delta S_c)\ge\sigma_c\), where \(f\) a homogeneous functional of degree 1.
  • A mathematical formulation of Principle of Minimum Total Energy subject to a Stress Condition: \(\begin{align} &\underbrace{\text{min}}_{\alpha \in A_\sigma}\quad \Pi(\Delta S_c(\alpha))+R(\Delta S_c(\alpha))\\ &\text{where the feasible paramete-region}\\ &A_\sigma={\alpha \in A|f(\sigma_ij(\cdot),\Delta S_c(\alpha))\ge\sigma_c)}\subset A \subset \mathbb{R}^m \end{align} \)
[1] Vladislav Mantic, Keynote lecture at the 16th European Conference on Composite Materials (ECCM16), 2014

Contact problems

  • Contact problems often present in engineering applications (e.g. composites), represent one of the most important and interesting topics of mechanics.
  • Contact between deformable bodies is a complex and inherently non-linear problem.
  • It is essentially a boundary phenomenon which has a strong effect on stress and displacement fields in the vicinity of the boundary of the contacting bodies.
  • Phenomenologically one of the simplest models of contact is given by the Signorini contact condition, that imposes the strict non-penetrability of the bodies in contact.

Numerical Solution as minimization of total potential energy problem

We consider the case of linearized elasticity and assume that the elastic strain energy \(\mathscr{E}\) stored in the volume \(\Omega\) is
\(\begin{align} \mathscr{E}(u)=\sum_{i=1}^N\int_{\Omega^{i}}\!\frac{1}{2}\mathbb C^{(i)}e^i(u^i){:}e^i(u^i)\,\mathrm{d} \Omega \end{align}\)
We define the stored energy functional wich coincides with the total potential energy,
\(\begin{align} \Pi(t,u)=\!\left\{ \begin{array}{ll} \mathscr{E}(u)-\mathscr{W}(t,u) &\text{if }u=u_{\text{D}}\text{ on }\Gamma_{\text{D}},\\[.0em] &[u]_{\text{n}}\ge-g_n\text{ on }\Gamma_{\text{C}}, \\[.3em] \infty& \text{elsewhere,} \end{array}\right. \end{align}\)
where, \(\mathscr{W}(t,u)=\sum_{i=1}^N\int_{\Gamma_{\text{N}}}u^i(x,t)\cdot p_{\text{N}}^i (x,t)\,\mathrm{d} S\).
A process \(u(t)\) is stable for \(\Pi\) at time \(t \in [0,T]\), if
\( \begin{align} \Pi(t,u(t))<+\infty \text{ and}\\ \Pi(t,u)\leq \Pi(t,\tilde{u}(t)) \quad \forall \, \tilde{u}(t) \in \mathscr{U} \end{align} \)

Boundary Element Method

We express the energy principles in boundary integral forms in order to state the problem as a boundary integral quadratic one and take advantage of the Boundary Element Method.
\( \begin{align} \Pi (t,u_{\text{C}})= \frac{1}{2}\int_{\Gamma_{\text{C}}}u_{\text{C}} \mathscr{M}_{\text{CC}}u_{\text{C}} \,\mathrm{d}S+\int_{\Gamma_{\text{C}}}u_{\text{C}} \Big(\mathscr{M}_{\text{CD}}u_{\text{D}}(t){+}\mathscr{M}_{\text{CN}}p_\text{N}(t) \Big) \,\mathrm{d}S \end{align} \)
The total potential energy for the assemblage of $N$ bodies is defined as follows,
\( \begin{align} \Pi(t,u_{\text{C}})=\sum_{i=1}^{N} \Pi^{i}(t,u_{\text{C}}^{i}). \end{align} \)
After suitable discretization, the above equation may be given in the commonly defined algebraic form of a quadratic problem, that is:
\( \begin{align} f(\xi)=\frac{1}{2}\xi^T A \xi - \xi^T b. \end{align} \)
where \(A\) a square matrix while \(b,\xi\) vectors.

(2/3) Shape optimization for idiophone musical instruments

- Th. Krioneritis thesis in Technological Educational Institute of Crete (ongoing, supervision)

Brief description

  • We use sensitivity analysis and model updating techniques taking advantage of the Finite Element Method in order to design bars of a marimba musical instrument with specific overtones.
  • That means we define the shape of the bar in order to control its modal characteristics such as higher eigenfrequencies (usually the \(\omega_2\) and \(\omega_3\)) to have specific ratios with respect to the fundamental one. That is for the i\(^\text{th}\) eigenfrequency \(\omega_i\) to have the ratio \(\frac{\omega_i}{\omega_1}=r_i\)

  • We solve the problem with identical techniques with what we previously presented for the case of model updating of the Egyptian lighthouse of Rethymno
  • The difference between these two cases is that in the lighthouse case we use model updating for identification purposes while in the marimba case for design purposes.
The work is in progress, in discussion and partly collaboration with prof. S. Kouzoupis (Department of Music Technology and Acoustics)

(3/3) Symplegma

- C.G. Panagiotopoulos, Symplegma, a JVM implementation for numerical methods in computational mechanics. Free & Open-Source Software Communities Meeting (FOSSCOMM 2018), Heraklion, Greece, October 13-14, 2018 (oral , https://fosscomm2018.gr/fs2018/talk/7FXTCV/ ).

Symplegma components

A Java/Groovy framework for numerical methods and computational mechanics in order to develop an integrated toolkit appropriate for:
  • Teaching: The feature of close integration between computational mechanics theory and computer implementation of this toolkit helps a student to understand basic concepts of the computational methods,
  • Researching: Helps researchers quickly develop and test their models, experiment data and algorithms.
  • Engineering: The performance and efficiency may be unsatisfied for industrial applications, however it is possible to use it for solution of specific problems of limited size.
Basic components are:
  • >Climax: A Java library for computational mechanics inluding BEM, FEM, FDM etc.
  • >SDE: An integrated working and development environment for the CLIMAX libary and similar components.
Project's webpage at: http://symplegma.org/
Project's blog at: https://symplegma.blogspot.com/
